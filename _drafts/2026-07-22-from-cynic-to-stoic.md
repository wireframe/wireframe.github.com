## Cleanup on Aisle 2003

Last week was a rough one.  A catastrophic failure in our basement plumbing, and my birthday spent cleaning and salvaging anything that could be saved.

Still in that clean-out mood, I figured I'd do some housekeeping on my blog, and fix a few dead links and prune some tags.  Seeing twenty-plus years of posts get a touch-up, I stopped on a question I'd somehow never asked: How do the changes in my writing over time reflect my changes as an individual?

So I asked my Claude coach to roll up its sleeves and go to work.  Read everything, analyze the content and infer changes in my tone, voice, identity, values, and world-view.  And to be _brutally_ honest. 

## The Eras Tour (Sonnek's Version)

What came back was a surprise to say the least.  Twenty years of writing distilled into three distinct eras with meaningful differences in the written tone, individual identity, and underlying beliefs.

These eras map reasonably cleanly to changes in roles in my professional career progressing from "coder" to "leader", but there was more nuance found in this one-liner:

> The old self was a curious, defended perfectionist: hungry for ideas, distrustful of people, ego fused to being right. The current self is a curious, open builder: the same appetite for ideas, but ability is now malleable, being wrong is information, and talent is assumed to be everywhere. 

## The Candid Mirror

I sat with this analysis for quite some time.  Claude did not pull any punches.  Who was that "Ranter" version of me? I barely recognized him, and he was not kind.  I viewed the world as black and white...me versus everyone else.  The "other people" who [didn't "get it"]({% post_url 2005-10-10-real-world %}), and the "[mindless drones]({% post_url 2006-03-20-curse-of-passion %})." I told frustrated developers they should "[consider tossing yourself from a balcony]({% post_url 2006-06-14-when-will-hurting-stop %})." I called software that I didn't like "[one of the greatest crimes against humanity I have witnessed in recent history]({% post_url 2010-01-10-airfail-for-html-developers %})."  When someone struggled with software I'd built, [I blamed the user]({% post_url 2023-11-23-my-no-shame-november-story %}) instead of identifying the bugs in my own platform.

What was the source of The Ranter's cynicism?  I've found this explanation from Peter Senge to resonate strongly:

> Scratch the surface of most cynics and you find a frustrated idealist, someone who made the mistake of converting his ideals into expectations.  - Peter Senge (The Fifth Discipline)

That hit the nail right on the head. I was an idealist who turned strong opinions into unspoken expectations on everyone else, and then got worked-up when they behaved differently.  Ego and pride taking the driver's seat assuming my opinions were the "right" ones.

## The Shift

So what changed?  I can't point to a specific thing, but I do believe there are a few forces pulling the same direction over a lot of years.

The obvious ones came from the outside. I got married in 2004. Kids arrived not long after. My career walked me from writing code alone, to leading a team, to leading leaders. Each step widened the aperture. It's hard to keep sorting the world into people who "get it" when my whole job becomes helping other people get there, and hard to stay at war with a world I'm busy building a family.

The non-obvious ones was the work going on inside. I stopped handing my peace of mind to other people. The Ranter was angry because he had wired his sense of being right to having control over how everyone else behaved. Bad code, a user who didn't understand my elegant system, a coworker who didn't "get it": all of it was his to be furious about. Somewhere in the last decade I stopped picking up that bill. I own what I build and how I show up. I can't own whether the world agrees with me, and I finally quit trying to.

None of that internal work was self-taught. I've spent more than ten years at BetterUp, which means I've had a dedicated coach for most of them, and regular time with a good coach has done more to reshape how I show up than almost anything else I can point to. More recently AI pulled up a chair too; this analysis is a good example. A coach doesn't hand you the answer. They ask the question that makes you notice the bill you keep picking up, and then help you set it down.

The change didn't happen overnight.  There was no "lightbulb" moment.  It was slow and arduous, because the beliefs that underpin the behaviors take time to rewire.  Research in Positive Psychology by Jer Clifton created an an assessment that measures your gut-level beliefs about the world, the kind that hold fairly steady across a lifetime. Two of my core beliefs moved materially over these eras. I stopped believing the world was **dangerous**, and I started believing it was **good**.

The early blog is a low-safety world with enemies everywhere: bad frameworks, worse managers, the drones. I had to fight and prove myself to do anything worthwhile. The recent writing isn't naive about danger, but the threat moved. It points at complacency and bureaucracy now, not at people. Over time, the world stopped feeling like a fight I had to win and started feeling like a place that was basically on my side.

Put all of that together and it has a name. The Stoics called it owning your conditions instead of chasing the outcome. For me that meant holding onto what was mine, the work and the effort and how I treated people, and letting go of what wasn't, like whether the world agreed. Being wrong stopped being a wound and turned into information that I could use to improve. Holding the ideals and dropping the demands.

## The Cleanup Continues

The basement is dry now, but the cleanup continues in the house, and in me.  I'm still a work in progress and will be for many years to come.

I'm grateful for the chance to look back, and for the people and tools that helped me do the work.  If you're interested, I highly recommend [the Primals assessment](https://myprimals.com) for understanding your current world-view, and the [Positive Intelligence Saboteurs assessment](https://www.positiveintelligence.com/saboteurs/) for understanding how you show up when you are _not_ at your best.

Keep mopping!
