# How You Changed: 23 Years of the Blog as a Mirror

*A private report for Ryan. Not for publication. It reads the archive (290 posts, 2003–2026) as evidence of how your tone, identity, beliefs, and world-view shifted over time. Every quote is verbatim from a post and cited. The candor is deliberate: you asked for an honest mirror, so the regressions and blind spots are named alongside the growth.*

## The three eras

The posting volume, more than the calendar, marks the seasons of this blog. Three eras fall out of the data:

| Era | Years | Posts | Who you were |
|---|---|---|---|
| **The Ranter** | 2003–2007 | 169 | Combative in-the-trenches Java coder, day-job frustrated, side-project prolific |
| **The Builder** | 2008–2014 | 104 | Rails startup convert and community organizer, then IC→tech-lead; "my code" becomes "our code" |
| **The Leader** | 2015–2026 | 17 | Senior eng/product leader at BetterUp; org, culture, AI |

An earlier cut split the first stretch in two, with a separate "Evangelist" era (2007–2009). The data doesn't support it. In 2007 you were still 96% Java (24 of 25 posts), indistinguishable from the Ranter years; the Java→Ruby pivot actually runs 2008–2011, straddling that boundary; and the combative tone continues to about 2010. So 2007 belongs with the Ranter, and the startup/Rails pivot is the front of a single Builder era that runs through the 2013 tech-lead hinge. Cadence was the only thing that set 2007–2009 apart, and cadence is not identity.

The blog went quiet after 2015. There were zero posts from 2016 through 2021, then one each in 2022 and 2023, before it came back in 2024 as a leadership and strategy platform. The genre break is sharp. In 2015 you were still shipping shell-script protips. By 2024 you were writing about scaling a 100-engineer organization.

## Tone and voice

The early voice is combative and unfiltered. You told JSP users to "consider tossing yourself from a balcony" (`2006-06-14-when-will-hurting-stop.md`), called Adobe AIR "one of the greatest crimes against humanity I have witnessed in recent history" (`2010-01-10-airfail-for-html-developers.md`), and dismissed a peer's approach as "another example of people that just don't 'get it'" (`2005-10-10-real-world.md`). ALL-CAPS, exclamation points, and uncorrected typos ("suckyness," "guarentee") set the register. This was fast, angry, and unedited on purpose.

The current voice keeps the punch and drops the cruelty. You still open with an anecdote, still ask the reader direct questions, still close with a call to action. What changed is where the authority comes from. Where you once asserted correctness by insulting the alternative, you now borrow it from named sources (Cagan, Drucker, Iger, Clear) and hedge with genuine humility: "Every mental model is wrong…some are useful. Take this template if it helps, leave it if it doesn't" (`2025-02-04-v2ooo.md`). The joy is still there too: "Programming is __fun__ again… What a time to be alive" (`2025-08-25-hacker-renaissance.md`).

The through-line across all 23 years: opinionated, anecdote-first, allergic to bureaucracy, and unable to end a post without telling the reader to go do something.

## Identity: coder to leader

The role you write from tracks your career almost exactly, and the clearest signal is the *size of the thing you care about*.

In the Ranter era you wrote as a hands-on developer with a frustrating corporate day-job and a stack of side projects. You called yourself "A Passionate Opensource Software Developer" (`2006-03-20-curse-of-passion.md`), and the unit of concern was a method, a framework, a gem.

The Builder era opens with the pivot. You left corporate Java for a Rails startup: "I'll finally be able to dive into the world of apple and use a macbook pro as my primary development platform" (`2008-04-21-open-wide.md`). You organized user groups, shipped your own gem, ran a hack-a-thon. Still an individual contributor, but now a public one.

The same era's back half is where you became a lead. The 2013 hinge matters most: leaving Socialcast for "the unknown," you wrote "If you are unwilling to change, you are *guaranteed* to atrophy" (`2013-07-16-a-new-day.md`). Around here "my gem" becomes "we built," personal repos move to an organization you run, and you start writing about a "team of 25 developers" (`2014-09-10-optimized-development-workflow.md`) and mentoring "developers of the future" (`2011-01-14-3-es-of-engineering.md`).

By the Leader era the unit of concern is an entire engineering organization and its culture. You write about assigning system ownership "to a *team* instead of an individual" (`2024-03-22-scaling-platform-quality.md`) and declare that "You Don't Declare a Culture — You Engineer One" (`2026-05-30-engineering-your-remote-engineering-team.md`). The progression is legible in one line: a method, then a team's lifecycle, then an org's culture.

## Beliefs and values

Some convictions never moved. Testing as non-negotiable runs from "Untested code is broken code" in 2005 (`2005-10-05-untested-equals-broken.md`) straight through to how you frame quality work in 2024. Clarity over cleverness ("Give me clean _slow_ code any day," `2006-04-13-performance-is-overrated.md`) becomes "Craftsmanship has no upper bound" (`2024-04-13-the-commit-cycle.md`). Ship small, tolerate imperfection, distrust bureaucracy. All constant.

What did change is how tightly you hold a belief. The absolutist who ranked people by whether they "get it" later writes: "I'm all for separation of concerns, but this is a simple matter of pragmatism vs dogma" (`2014-11-17-rspec-controllers-best-practices.md`). You also got comfortable being publicly wrong, recanting Adobe AIR (`2009-08-03-end-of-adobe-air.md`) and reframing dependency injection as a testability crutch rather than gospel (`2008-08-09-end-of-dependency-injection_09.md`).

The sharpest reversal is about people, not code. See the next section.

## World-view: fixed to growth (with one important caveat)

You suspected your old self held a fixed mindset and your current self holds a growth mindset. The archive supports that, and it sharpens it.

**The headline evidence is a direct reversal in your own words.** In 2005 you sorted the world into people who "get it" and people who don't (`2005-10-10-real-world.md`): talent as innate, people as sortable by it. In 2026 you wrote: "Talent is equally distributed. Opportunity is not" (`2026-05-30-engineering-your-remote-engineering-team.md`). Talent went from a fixed property some people simply have to a universal input gated by circumstance. The same turn shows up with users: you once dismissed them with "PEBCAK… my term of choice to talk down on others who didn't understand the elegance of systems I was building," then named that habit as shame and pivoted to customer empathy (`2023-11-23-my-no-shame-november-story.md`).

**The caveat keeps this honest.** "Fixed to growth" is true, but not about everything. You were always growth-minded about *skills and technology*. Even the 2003 posts show relentless self-teaching and constant framework-hopping. The fixed mindset lived in a narrower place: how you judged *other people's* potential, and whether your self-worth depended on being right. In the Ranter era, being correct was identity, and disagreement read as someone else's stupidity. That is what actually flipped. The curiosity was never fixed; the ego was.

**How the world-view moved, early self versus now.** The mindset shift is one piece of a larger change in how the world itself looks to you. The clearest way to see it is to ask the questions a person answers with their gut, not their opinions: is the world good, is it safe, is it interesting, is it alive and responsive? Read across the whole archive, two of those answers moved a lot and two barely moved at all.

*Safe* moved the most. The early blog is a low-safety world with adversaries everywhere: JSP, vendors, management, the "drones." You had to fight and prove yourself to do good work, and the writing has its guard up. The recent writing is not naive about danger, but the threat changed address. It now points at complacency, bureaucracy, and RTO mandates rather than at people. You write like someone safe enough to be wrong in public.

*Good* moved with it. Early on, the good was real but scarce and embattled: craft and open source were beautiful, and much of what surrounded them was contemptible. The current default is that the world is basically good and that ambiguity probably hides something worth having. The starting assumption flipped from suspicion to good intent.

*Interesting* never moved. This is the constant engine of the whole archive. From "every programmer is an artist" in 2004 (`2004-03-20-why-opensource-exists.md`) to "Programming is __fun__ again… What a time to be alive" in 2025 (`2025-08-25-hacker-renaissance.md`), the world has always looked to you like a place full of things worth exploring.

*Alive and responsive* also held, but its source shifted. Early, the belief that engagement matters showed up as raw individual will ("change the world, right here, right now") and, by 2009, as explicit faith in a purposeful world. Today it shows up as a leader's conviction that conditions and systems respond to deliberate design. The belief that the world answers back stayed; the account of why it answers changed from personal will, to faith, to systems.

So the arc is not that you became an optimist. The curiosity and the sense that the world is alive were there from the first year. What changed is that the world stopped feeling like a fight. It got safer and more trustworthy, and the guard came down. A 2023 assessment you took (the Primal World Beliefs survey) marks where this landed: an optimist-explorer who reads the world as interesting and alive, treats it as basically good, and stays most guarded on whether it is safe. That is the same shape the writing traces. The one honest limit is that it is a single recent datapoint; every earlier-era read here is inferred from the posts, not measured.

**A temperament read: angry idealist to faith-grounded Stoic.** A shorter way to name the same shift. The early voice reads as cynical about *people* (the "drones," the sneering at those who don't "get it") while staying fiercely idealistic about *the work* ("change the world, right here, right now"). That is not a true cynic, who is detached and resigned; it is an angry idealist with a cynical streak. The current voice is Stoic in temperament: control the conditions rather than demand the outcome ("leaders own conditions, not outcomes"), treat being wrong as information ("every mental model is wrong…some are useful"), and put duty to others above ego. One correction to an easy assumption: this is not a Stoic posture bolted on top of a separate Christian foundation, as if the two pulled against each other. Stoicism was never a secular philosophy. Its God is real and load-bearing: the *logos*, the divine reason pervading nature, a providence the Stoics urged you to live "in accord with." Seneca calls God a father who loves us (*On Providence*), Epictetus thanks God on nearly every page, Marcus addresses "the gods" throughout. And Christianity has drawn on Stoicism from the start. Paul on the Areopagus quotes the Stoic poetic tradition ("in him we live and move and have our being," Acts 17:28); the church fathers (Augustine in *City of God*, Ambrose, Tertullian) reworked Stoic *apatheia* into Christian consolation; the Renaissance Neostoics under Justus Lipsius rebuilt Stoicism explicitly for a Christian audience. "Christian Stoic" is an old and coherent lineage. The label fits you because the Stoics were men of faith, not in spite of it.

The genuine seam is finer, and worth naming precisely. The classical Stoic's higher power is impersonal and immanent: God *is* the rational cosmos, and fate is something you align with and accept. The Christian's God is personal and transcendent, distinct from creation, in covenant, offering grace rather than bare necessity, and affirming the unique worth of each person (a value strict Stoicism subordinates to the good of the whole). You take the Stoic practice — the dichotomy of control, virtue over outcome, gratitude, owning the conditions — and ground it in a personal, named God rather than an impersonal principle. The Stoics kept pointing at a higher power; you know His name. That is where the temperament label needs its one footnote, and the footnote resolves toward continuity, not tension.

The temperament arc and the fixed-to-growth arc are the same motion seen from two angles: a defended, judging stance giving way to an accepting, disciplined one.

This is not only an outside read. Your own notes make the same diagnosis. You have collected Senge's line that "scratch the surface of most cynics and you find a frustrated idealist, someone who made the mistake of converting his ideals into expectations," and filed it under the idea that cynicism comes from a lack of belief and trust. That describes the early voice precisely: high ideals about craft and testing, converted into expectations of everyone around you, curdling into contempt when they went unmet. The same notes name the antidote you now practice: staying clear and kind, having the courage to say what you mean, and choosing hope over the easier, self-soothing posture of cynicism. The anti-cynicism move and the Stoic move turn out to be one move. Hold the ideal, and drop the demand that the world comply with it.

## The candid mirror

You asked for the unflattering parts named, so here they are, grounded in the text.

**The early cruelty had targets, and some were people.** Telling strangers to jump off a balcony is funny until you remember real developers read it. The "mindless drones" framing (`2005-10-10-real-world.md`) was contempt aimed at colleagues, not just tools. Your own later post admits the cost of this stance toward users (`2023-11-23-my-no-shame-november-story.md`).

**Certainty was your default, and it wasn't always earned.** The Ranter years, and the front of the Builder era, treated strong opinions as proof of competence ("You've got to be confident if you really believe you're right," `2006-03-20-curse-of-passion.md`). The pragmatism came later. Some of the early crusades (the multi-year war on JSP, for instance) read now as a young engineer mistaking volume for rigor.

**The faith thread left the writing, not your life.** In 2008–2009 your Christian faith was explicit and load-bearing in how you made decisions: "Know that the outcome is in God's hands" (`2009-01-28-3-keys-to-decision-making.md`); passion as something you "was built with… as one of the strongest fibers in my being," redirected toward faith (`2008-11-28-my-strongest-strength.md`). By the Leader era it is nearly absent from the public writing. This is the one place the text alone would mislead an outside reader: the archive shows faith fading from the page, but you've noted it has only deepened over these years. The change is editorial, not spiritual. Worth stating plainly, because a reader working from the posts alone would draw the wrong conclusion.

**Two measurement caveats.** First, the Primals scores are one datapoint from 2023; treat the per-era world-view arc as a reasoned read, not data. Second, the eras are wildly unequal in size (169 posts versus 17), so the early reads rest on sampling while the late era was read in full. The confidence in each claim should scale accordingly.

## The one-line version

The old self was a curious, defended perfectionist: hungry for ideas, distrustful of people, ego fused to being right. The current self is a curious, open builder: the same appetite for ideas, but ability is now malleable, being wrong is information, and talent is assumed to be everywhere. The clearest proof is not a change in mood. It is a specific sentence you reversed: "people who don't get it" (2005) became "talent is equally distributed" (2026).
